# Impeccable Design Context — MenuQR

## Design Context

### Users
**Primary surface**: The customer-facing menu, accessed by scanning a QR code at a restaurant table.

- **Who**: Diners at Indian restaurants — mixed age range, mostly on phones, often in a noisy/dim environment
- **Context**: At the table, probably hungry, may be with others. They want to browse and order quickly, without friction
- **Job to be done**: Find dishes they want, add them to cart, submit order — ideally in under a minute
- **Secondary surface**: Restaurant owners configure menus via the dashboard (not the primary design focus)

### Color System
Each restaurant brings its own color palette, generated from their logo via [stitch.withgoogle.com](https://stitch.withgoogle.com). These map into the `MenuTokens` system in `src/lib/tokens.ts` (`--sunday-primary`, `--sunday-accent`, etc.).

**Implication**: Components must look exceptional across ANY color palette — warm amber, deep navy, forest green, terracotta. The design language lives in:
- Typography choices and hierarchy
- Spatial rhythm and spacing scale
- Motion and interaction patterns
- Component structure and shape

Color tokens are a variable input, not the design foundation.

### Brand Personality
**Three words**: Polished, confident, alive

- Not the generic "clean and minimal" — this should feel like a real, well-crafted app
- Not loud or decorative — the restaurant's food and brand should take center stage
- The interface earns trust by feeling native and precise, not by trying to impress

### Aesthetic Direction
**App-like, native feel** — the benchmark is a well-designed iOS or Android app, not a website

- Smooth, purposeful gesture-style interactions (sheets slide in, items snap in with stagger)
- Tight, intentional spacing — every gap is deliberate
- Typography carries hierarchy independently of color (since color is variable)
- Components feel tactile: tap states, feedback, momentum
- No web-page affordances: no big hero sections, no generic card grids, no hover-first patterns

**Theme**: Light or dark is restaurant-controlled via their token palette — components must work in both

**Anti-references**:
- Generic Zomato/Swiggy clones — too dense, too commodity
- Glassmorphism, glowing accents, gradient text — banned
- Identical card grids where every item looks the same weight
- Anything that would make someone say "this is just another QR menu"

### Emotion Goal
**Impressed & delighted** — the customer should feel the restaurant cared enough to give them something beautiful. The polish is what creates trust: if the menu feels this good, the food probably is too.

### Design Principles

1. **Palette-agnostic by design** — Every component must look intentional with warm amber, cool navy, or anything between. Typography weight, spatial contrast, and motion carry the hierarchy — not color alone.

2. **Native-app confidence** — No web-page awkwardness. Touch targets are generous, sheets feel physical, scrolling is frictionless. Interactions give immediate tactile feedback.

3. **Delight lives in the details** — Staggered entrances, precise type scale, micro-feedback on add-to-cart. The customer shouldn't be able to name what makes it feel good — it should just feel right.

4. **Typography is structure** — Since the palette varies per restaurant, the type system must establish visual hierarchy on its own. Fewer sizes, more contrast between them. Weight and spacing over color differentiation.

5. **Coherent motion language** — One easing curve family, consistent durations, entrances that feel like the interface arriving rather than elements appearing. Sheet transitions, card reveals, and state changes should feel like a family.
